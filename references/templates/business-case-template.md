### 4.2 Requirement Prioritization (MoSCoW)

> Use the MoSCoW method to rigorously prioritize requirements within the solution scope, ensuring investment focuses on core value.

| Level | Requirement/Feature | Proportion | Rationale |
|------|----------|------|------|
| **Must Have** | [List] | ≤60% | Without these, the solution cannot deliver core value |
| **Should Have** | [List] | ≤20% | Important but not critical; workarounds can compensate |
| **Could Have** | [List] | ≤15% | Nice-to-have; do if resources permit |
| **Won't Have** | [List] | Explicitly excluded | Not in this phase; deferred to future versions/phases |

**MoSCoW Decision Basis:**
- Is the Must Have proportion ≤60%? If exceeded, re-examine the definition of "must"
- Has a follow-up plan been defined for Won't Have items? Avoid being misinterpreted as "never doing"
- Has the prioritization been confirmed by core stakeholders (Sponsor + Business Owner)?

## v1.1.0 Added: MoSCoW Prioritization Analysis

### Requirement Priority Matrix
| Priority | Definition | Recommended Proportion | Consequence of Not Implementing |
|--------|------|---------|------------|
| **Must Have** | Must implement; otherwise project fails | 60% | Project cannot be delivered |
| **Should Have** | Should implement; important but not critical | 20% | User experience degraded |
| **Could Have** | Can implement; nice-to-have | 15% | Minor impact |
| **Won't Have** | Not this time; consider for future | 5% | No impact |

### MoSCoW Decision Rules
1. Must Have ≤ 60% of total requirements (exceeding indicates unclear scope definition)
2. Every requirement must have a clear classification rationale
3. Won't Have does not mean "not important"; it means "not this time"
4. Classification must be jointly confirmed by stakeholders
